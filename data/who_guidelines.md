# WHO-Style Primary Healthcare Guidelines for Rural Settings

## SECTION 1: EMERGENCY CONDITIONS — IMMEDIATE REFERRAL REQUIRED

### 1.1 Chest Pain and Cardiac Events
Chest pain, especially with radiation to the left arm, jaw, or back, should be treated as a cardiac emergency until proven otherwise. Symptoms of heart attack include crushing chest pain, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, and dizziness. Any patient presenting with chest pain should be urgently referred to the nearest emergency facility. Do not have the patient walk; use a vehicle. Keep patient calm and seated. Do not give food or water. Aspirin 325mg can be given if available and not contraindicated.

### 1.2 Stroke
Signs of stroke include sudden facial drooping, arm weakness, and speech difficulty (FAST: Face, Arm, Speech, Time). Other signs: sudden numbness, vision changes, severe headache, loss of balance. Any patient with suspected stroke must reach a hospital within 3–4.5 hours for best outcomes. Do not give anything by mouth. Keep airway clear.

### 1.3 Severe Breathing Difficulty
Patients unable to speak in full sentences, using accessory muscles to breathe, showing cyanosis (blue lips/nails), or with oxygen saturation below 90% require immediate emergency care. Causes include severe asthma, pneumonia, pulmonary edema, anaphylaxis, and foreign body obstruction.

### 1.4 Unconsciousness and Seizures
Unconscious patients should be placed in recovery position if breathing. Seizures lasting more than 5 minutes or occurring in clusters are emergencies. Febrile seizures in children under 5 require urgent evaluation. Never restrain a seizing patient; protect from injury.

### 1.5 Obstetric Emergencies
Pregnancy with heavy vaginal bleeding, severe abdominal pain, or absent fetal movement is an emergency. Pre-eclampsia signs: severe headache, visual disturbances, upper abdominal pain, sudden face/hand swelling with high BP. Post-partum hemorrhage is the leading cause of maternal death; immediate referral required.

### 1.6 Poisoning and Overdose
Any suspected poisoning should be referred immediately. Do not induce vomiting unless specifically instructed by a medical professional. Note the substance involved and bring packaging.

## SECTION 2: HIGH URGENCY CONDITIONS — SAME-DAY EVALUATION NEEDED

### 2.1 High Fever (>39°C / 102°F)
High fever with rash may indicate dengue, measles, or meningococcal disease. Meningitis signs: neck stiffness, photophobia, severe headache with fever — urgent referral. Fever in children under 3 months is always urgent. Fever persisting beyond 72 hours without obvious cause needs evaluation. Fever with rigors in malaria-endemic areas is high urgency.

### 2.2 Dengue Fever
Warning signs for severe dengue: abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fluid accumulation, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, liver enlargement greater than 2cm, increase in hematocrit with rapid decrease in platelets. These signs indicate the critical phase and require immediate hospital admission.

### 2.3 Severe Dehydration
Signs of severe dehydration: absent urine output for 8+ hours, sunken eyes, dry mouth and tongue, skin turgor loss, altered consciousness, inability to drink. Oral rehydration solution (ORS) should be started immediately. IV fluids may be needed; refer to nearest facility.

### 2.4 Pediatric Danger Signs (WHO IMCI)
In children under 5, the following are danger signs requiring urgent care: inability to drink or breastfeed, vomiting everything, history of convulsions, lethargic or unconscious, stridor, severe malnutrition (visible wasting or edema), and severe chest indrawing.

### 2.5 Diabetic Emergencies
Hyperglycemic crisis (blood sugar >250 mg/dL with vomiting, deep breathing, fruity breath) is an emergency. Hypoglycemia (blood sugar <70 mg/dL) with altered consciousness: give sugar immediately; call for help. Diabetic foot infections with fever or spreading redness need same-day evaluation.

### 2.6 Hypertensive Urgency
Blood pressure >180/120 mmHg with headache, visual changes, or chest pain is a hypertensive crisis. Do not attempt rapid lowering at home without medical supervision.

## SECTION 3: MEDIUM URGENCY — CLINIC VISIT WITHIN 24 HOURS

### 3.1 Fever (37.5–39°C)
Most fevers in adults are viral and self-limiting. Use paracetamol (acetaminophen) for fever reduction. Maintain hydration. Seek care if fever lasts more than 3 days, if there is associated rash, joint pain, or if the patient is elderly, very young, or immunocompromised.

### 3.2 Respiratory Infections
Common cold: runny nose, mild sore throat, mild cough, no fever or low-grade fever. Management: rest, hydration, saline nasal rinse. Antibiotics are NOT indicated for viral URI. Lower respiratory infection signs: productive cough, fever, shortness of breath, chest pain on breathing — evaluate within 24 hours. Suspected tuberculosis (cough >2 weeks, night sweats, weight loss, hemoptysis) requires sputum testing.

### 3.3 Diarrhea and Gastroenteritis
Mild-to-moderate: start ORS (1 sachet in 1 litre of clean water), continue feeding. Zinc supplementation for children under 5. Refer if: bloody diarrhea, more than 10 stools in 24 hours, signs of dehydration, fever >38.5°C, patient is infant or elderly.

### 3.4 Urinary Tract Infection
Symptoms: burning urination, frequency, lower abdominal pain, cloudy or foul-smelling urine. Uncomplicated UTI in women: treat with trimethoprim or nitrofurantoin if available; high fluid intake. Men, children, and pregnant women with UTI always need evaluation. Upper UTI (pyelonephritis): fever, chills, flank pain — refer for parenteral antibiotics.

### 3.5 Skin Infections
Impetigo: honey-colored crusted lesions, usually on face — wash with soap, topical antibiotic if available. Cellulitis: spreading redness, warmth, swelling with fever — needs oral antibiotics and evaluation. Abscess: fluctuant swelling may need drainage; do not apply pressure.

## SECTION 4: LOW URGENCY — HOME MANAGEMENT WITH MONITORING

### 4.1 Common Cold
Caused by rhinovirus and other viruses. Symptoms: runny nose, nasal congestion, sneezing, mild sore throat, mild cough, no significant fever. Management is symptomatic: rest, hydration (warm water, soups), honey for cough in adults, saline nasal rinse. Antibiotics are ineffective. Return if symptoms worsen after day 7 or fever develops.

### 4.2 Mild Headache
Tension headache: bilateral, pressing sensation, no nausea or vomiting, not worsened by activity. Management: paracetamol, rest, hydration. Red flags requiring evaluation: sudden severe "thunderclap" headache, headache with fever and neck stiffness, headache following head injury, new headache in person over 50, progressive worsening.

### 4.3 Minor Cuts and Wounds
Clean wound with soap and water or saline. Apply antiseptic if available. Cover with clean dressing. Tetanus prophylaxis if vaccination not current and wound is deep or contaminated. Seek care if wound is deep, gaping, cannot be closed with simple dressing, or shows signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, pus, fever).

### 4.4 Mild Musculoskeletal Pain
RICE: Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation for acute sprains. Paracetamol or ibuprofen (if no contraindication) for pain relief. Avoid strenuous activity. Seek care if pain is severe, joint is unstable, or there is visible deformity.

### 4.5 Insect Bites
Most insect bites cause local reaction: redness, swelling, itching. Management: clean area, apply ice, oral antihistamine for itching. Signs of allergic reaction (hives, difficulty breathing, tongue swelling) — treat as emergency. In malaria-endemic areas, fever within 2 weeks of mosquito exposure needs evaluation.

## SECTION 5: MATERNAL AND CHILD HEALTH

### 5.1 Antenatal Care
Every pregnant woman should have at least 4 antenatal visits. First visit before 12 weeks: confirm pregnancy, measure BP and weight, iron-folic acid supplementation, screen for anemia, HIV, syphilis. Danger signs in pregnancy: heavy bleeding, severe headache, swelling of face and hands, blurred vision, no fetal movement, fever, painful urination, seizures.

### 5.2 Immunization Schedule (Government of India)
At birth: BCG, OPV-0, Hepatitis B. 6 weeks: DPT-1, OPV-1, IPV-1, Hep B-2, Hib-1, PCV-1, Rotavirus-1. 10 weeks: DPT-2, OPV-2, Hib-2, PCV-2, Rotavirus-2. 14 weeks: DPT-3, OPV-3, IPV-2, Hib-3, PCV-3, Rotavirus-3. 9–12 months: Measles-Rubella, JE-1 (endemic areas), Vitamin A. 16–24 months: DPT booster, OPV booster, MR-2, JE-2. 5–6 years: DPT booster. Keep all immunization records.

### 5.3 Newborn Care
Immediately after birth: dry and wrap, initiate breastfeeding within 1 hour, delay cord clamping 1–3 minutes, apply chlorhexidine to cord stump. Danger signs in newborn: not feeding well, convulsions, fast breathing >60/min, grunting, jaundice before 24 hours or persisting beyond 2 weeks, abnormal movements, bulging fontanelle.

### 5.4 Childhood Nutrition
Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months. Complementary feeding from 6 months while continuing breastfeeding until 2 years. Signs of severe acute malnutrition: visible wasting, MUAC <11.5 cm (red band), bilateral pitting edema. These children need urgent care. Vitamin A supplementation at 9 months and every 6 months until 5 years.

## SECTION 6: COMMON CHRONIC CONDITIONS IN RURAL INDIA

### 6.1 Tuberculosis
TB is the leading infectious disease killer in India. Pulmonary TB symptoms: cough lasting ≥2 weeks, blood in sputum, chest pain, night sweats, unexplained weight loss, low-grade fever. Diagnosis: sputum microscopy, GeneXpert. Treatment: 6-month DOTS regimen (HRZE for 2 months, HR for 4 months). Drug-resistant TB needs specialist referral. Household contacts should be screened. Children under 5 who are contacts should receive isoniazid preventive therapy.

### 6.2 Hypertension
Silent killer: most patients have no symptoms. Target BP: <140/90 mmHg (or <130/80 for high-risk). Lifestyle: salt restriction (<5g/day), weight management, physical activity 150 min/week, no tobacco or alcohol. First-line medications: amlodipine, enalapril, losartan, hydrochlorothiazide. Adherence is critical; never stop medications without consulting doctor.

### 6.3 Diabetes Mellitus Type 2
Symptoms: excessive thirst, frequent urination, blurred vision, fatigue, slow wound healing. Fasting blood glucose ≥126 mg/dL on two occasions confirms diagnosis. HbA1c ≥6.5% confirms diagnosis. Management: diet (low glycemic index, portion control), exercise, metformin first-line if no contraindication. Monitor blood glucose regularly. Annual eye, kidney, and foot examinations.

### 6.4 Malaria
Plasmodium falciparum causes severe malaria. Symptoms: cyclical fever with chills and rigors, headache, muscle aches. Severe malaria: altered consciousness, severe anemia, jaundice, dark urine, repeated convulsions. Diagnosis: rapid diagnostic test (RDT) or microscopy. Treatment: artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT). Primaquine for P. vivax to prevent relapse. Pregnant women: do not use primaquine in first trimester.

### 6.5 Anemia
Most common cause in India: iron deficiency. Symptoms: fatigue, pallor (conjunctiva, palms, nails), palpitations, shortness of breath on exertion. Severe anemia (Hb <7 g/dL): may require blood transfusion. Iron supplementation: ferrous sulfate 200mg twice daily for at least 3 months. Also treat cause (worms, diet). Folate deficiency and B12 deficiency anemia need specific replacement.

## SECTION 7: MENTAL HEALTH IN PRIMARY CARE

### 7.1 Depression
Key symptoms: persistent low mood for ≥2 weeks, loss of interest or pleasure, fatigue, changes in sleep and appetite, poor concentration, feelings of worthlessness, recurrent thoughts of death or suicide. Screening tool: PHQ-9. Management: psychosocial support, problem-solving therapy, antidepressants (fluoxetine, amitriptyline) if indicated. Always assess suicide risk.

### 7.2 Suicidal Ideation
Take all expressions of suicidal intent seriously. Ask directly and calmly about suicidal thoughts. Do not leave patient alone. Remove access to means. Refer immediately to mental health services or emergency department. Involve family support.

### 7.3 Alcohol Use Disorders
Hazardous drinking is common in rural areas. AUDIT screening tool for detection. Complications: liver disease, peripheral neuropathy, Wernicke encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency). Alcohol withdrawal can be life-threatening: tremors, seizures, delirium tremens. Brief counseling: advise reduction, provide reasons. Refer severe cases.

## SECTION 8: INFECTION PREVENTION AND CONTROL

### 8.1 Hand Hygiene
The single most effective measure to prevent infection transmission. Wash hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds: before touching patient, before clean/aseptic procedures, after body fluid exposure, after touching patient, after touching patient surroundings. Use alcohol-based hand rub when hands are not visibly soiled.

### 8.2 Safe Water and Sanitation
Boil water for at least 1 minute or use chlorination if clean water is unavailable. Store in covered clean containers. Maintain latrines at least 30m from water sources. Practice open defecation-free habits.

### 8.3 Waste Disposal
Segregate biomedical waste: sharps in puncture-proof containers, infectious waste in yellow bags. Never recap needles. Dispose of sharps containers when 3/4 full.

## APPENDIX: ORAL REHYDRATION SOLUTION (ORS) PREPARATION

To make ORS at home: dissolve 6 level teaspoons of sugar and 1/2 teaspoon of salt in 1 litre of clean water. Give small sips frequently. For adults: 200–400mL after each loose stool. For children: 50–100mL after each stool. If pre-packaged ORS is available, use it according to instructions on the packet.

## APPENDIX: EMERGENCY CONTACT GUIDANCE

In India, dial 108 for ambulance services (available in most states). For women in distress: 181 (Women's Helpline). Child helpline: 1098. Mental health helpline: iCall 9152987821. ASHA workers and ANM (Auxiliary Nurse Midwife) are the first point of contact in rural areas. District hospitals and Community Health Centres (CHCs) provide 24-hour emergency services.
